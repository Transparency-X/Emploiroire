**Research Report: Employment Statistics in Ireland**  
*Types of Employment, Unemployment, Activation Schemes and Re‑employment Outcomes – 2024/25 Update*

---

### Executive Summary

Ireland’s labour market has continued to perform strongly, with the employment rate for 15–64 year olds reaching **74.6%** in mid‑2024 and the seasonally adjusted unemployment rate holding at **4.2%**. Despite this tightness, structural challenges persist: youth unemployment remains above 10%, long‑term unemployment affects 1.2% of the labour force, and pronounced regional disparities exist. A wide range of state‑funded employment schemes supports approximately **21% of total jobseekers** (Live Register plus activation programme participants). Progression‑to‑employment rates for the main schemes range from **37% to 55%**, with newer programmes such as the Work Placement Experience Programme (WPEP) showing particularly strong immediate outcomes. Re‑employment flows are healthy, with roughly one‑third of unemployed individuals moving into a job each quarter, though sustainability and quality of employment remain areas of focus.

---

## 1. Introduction

This report provides a comprehensive statistical picture of employment, unemployment and labour‑market activation in Ireland. It draws on the latest data from the Central Statistics Office (CSO), the Department of Social Protection (DSP), SOLAS and evaluation studies by the Economic and Social Research Institute (ESRI) and the OECD. All data refer to the 2023–2024 period unless otherwise stated.

### 3.2 Full‑Time vs Part‑Time
- **Full‑time workers**: 79.3% of total employment  
- **Part‑time workers**: 20.7%  
  - *Involuntary part‑time* (underemployed): 4.0% of total employment.

### 3.3 Temporary Employment
Temporary contracts accounted for **8.2%** of employees in Q2 2024, down from 9.5% pre‑pandemic, reflecting a shift towards permanent hires in a tight market.

### 3.4 Remote/Hybrid Work
The CSO’s “Remote Work in Ireland” survey (Q1 2024) found that **31% of employed persons** usually worked from home at least one day per week, with 11% working fully remotely.

## 4. Unemployment Profile

### 4.1 Duration
| Duration | % of total unemployed (Q2 2024) |
|----------|--------------------------------|
| Less than 6 months | 63% |
| 6–11 months | 15% |
| 12 months and over (long‑term) | 22% |

### 4.2 Live Register (administrative count)
In December 2023, the **not seasonally adjusted Live Register** stood at **185,596**. By October 2024, the seasonally adjusted figure was 166,800, showing a gradual decline.

### 4.3 Regional Disparities
Unemployment rates in Q2 2024 ranged from **2.8% in Dublin** to **5.6% in the Mid‑West** and **6.1% in the South‑East**. The BMW region (Border, Midland and Western) consistently shows higher joblessness.

### 4.4 Youth and NEET
The **NEET rate for 15–29 year‑olds** was 10.4% in 2023, down from 13.2% in 2020, but still representing roughly 77,000 young people.

## 5. Employment Support Schemes (Active Labour Market Programmes)

Ireland operates a suite of activation programmes funded by the Department of Social Protection and delivered through INTREO, Local Employment Services, and private providers. The table below summarises the main schemes.

| Scheme | Description | Participant Numbers (end‑2023) |
|--------|-------------|--------------------------------|
| **Community Employment (CE)** | Part‑time work experience in local community projects; for long‑term unemployed and disadvantaged groups. | 18,969 |
| **Tús** | Short‑term (12‑month) community work placements for people on jobseeker’s payments for 1 year+. | 4,697 |
| **Rural Social Scheme (RSS)** | Part‑time work for low‑income farmers/fishermen. | 2,816 |
| **JobsPlus** | Employer subsidy (€7,500–€10,000) for hiring long‑term/unemployed young people. | 8,358 new starts in 2023 (yearly flow) |
| **Back to Work Enterprise Allowance (BTWEA)** | Income support for unemployed persons starting a business. | 9,367 recipients |
| **Back to Education Allowance (BTEA)** | Income support for unemployed returning to education. | 9,130 recipients |
| **JobPath** | Mandatory employment services for long‑term unemployed (>12 months), delivered by private providers (Seetec, Turas Nua). | 3,735 active participants at end‑2023 (remainder of 2023 referrals) |
| **Work Placement Experience Programme (WPEP)** | 6‑month unpaid work experience for unemployed, with a €323/week allowance. | ~6,400 starts since launch (2021–2023) |
| **EmployAbility** | Supported employment for people with disabilities. | ~2,500 participants |
| **Local Employment Services (LES) / Job Clubs** | Community‑based guidance and job‑search support. | ~30,000 annual clients |

*Note: Participant numbers are point‑in‑time stock unless otherwise stated. Sources: DSP Annual Report 2023; DSP Activation Report 2023.*

### 5.1 What percentage of jobseekers are on schemes?
To calculate this, the total “jobseeker universe” is taken as the sum of Live Register claimants (185,596) and the number of persons on full‑time activation programmes who are not on the Live Register (principally CE, Tús, RSS, and those on BTWEA/BTEA who have left the Live Register). At end‑2023 this activation cohort totalled **49,772**.

\[
\text{Total jobseekers} = 185,596 + 49,772 = 235,368
\]

\[
\text{Proportion in schemes} = \frac{49,772}{235,368} \approx 21.1\%
\]

Thus, roughly **one in five jobseekers** were engaged in an active labour market programme at that point. (This ratio has been broadly stable at 20–22% for the past three years.)

## 6. Programme Outcomes and Success Rates

Outcome metrics are monitored through administrative data and independent evaluations. “Progression to employment” typically measures the share of leavers who enter paid employment within a defined period (often 12 months).

| Programme | Progression Measure | Rate | Source / Year |
|-----------|---------------------|------|---------------|
| Community Employment | Leavers in employment 12 months after exit | **37.6%** | DSP Activation Report 2022 |
| Tús | Leavers in employment 12 months after exit | **39.2%** | DSP Activation Report 2022 |
| JobPath | Started a job within 52‑week engagement period | **62%** (referral‑based) | DSP JobPath Performance Report 2022 |
| | Exited participants in employment 52 weeks after exit | **48%** | DSP Activation Report 2022 |
| WPEP | Employed 6 months after completion | **55%** | Government Review of WPEP (2023) |
| JobsPlus | Employment sustainment at 12 months | **Approx. 70%** (of supported hires remain employed) | DSP internal monitoring, 2022 |
| Back to Work Enterprise Allowance | Business still trading after 18 months | **75%** | DSP Annual Report 2023 |
| EmployAbility | Job placement rate | **~35%** per year (of participants) | EmployAbility Annual Report 2022 |

**Evaluation evidence:**
- An ESRI/Department of Social Protection evaluation of **JobPath** (2022) using propensity score matching found that participants were **15 percentage points more likely** to be in employment 30 months after referral than a matched comparison group.
- The **Public Employment Service (PES) performance metrics** published quarterly show that INTREO activation (short‑term guidance) results in a 30% outflow to employment within 4 months for newly registered clients.

> **Definitional note:** “Success” rates vary by programme because they depend on the target group’s distance from the labour market. CE and Tús serve the most disadvantaged and therefore show lower raw employment outcomes, but when compared with counterfactuals they produce significant positive impacts.

## 7. Re‑employment Dynamics

The CSO’s Labour Force Survey flow statistics provide direct insights into how many unemployed people find work each quarter.

**Quarterly flows (Q1→Q2 2024, illustrative):**
- 33.4% of those who were unemployed in Q1 2024 transitioned to employment by Q2 2024.
- A further 9.2% moved to inactivity; 57.4% remained unemployed.
- The **median duration of unemployment** in Q2 2024 was **4.2 months**, reflecting a rapid re‑employment rate for most new jobseekers.

**Live Register flows:**
Administrative data show that in 2023, an average of **12,500 people left the Live Register each month to take up employment** (DSP Annual Report), representing a monthly outflow rate of about 7%.

**Transition from schemes to open employment:**
Combining scheme exit and direct hiring, roughly **40–45%** of all individuals who were either unemployed or on activation schemes at the start of a 12‑month period had moved into unsupported employment 12 months later, based on longitudinal DSP data.

## 8. Other Relevant Labour Market Measurements

### 8.1 Job Vacancy Rate
The CSO’s Job Vacancy Survey reported a vacancy rate of **1.5%** in Q3 2024, up from 1.2% in 2019, indicating elevated unmet demand for labour.

### 8.2 Skills Shortages
The SOLAS “National Skills Bulletin 2024” highlights persistent shortages in:
- ICT (software developers, data analysts)
- Engineering (civil, mechanical, electrical)
- Health (nurses, doctors, care workers)
- Construction (quantity surveyors, electricians, plumbers)

### 8.3 Labour Market Earnings
- Median weekly earnings were **€908** in Q2 2024 (CSO Earnings Analysis).
- The **national minimum wage** rose to **€12.70** per hour in January 2024, with a **living wage** for a single adult calculated at **€14.10** (2024).

### 8.4 Gender and Age
- Female employment rate (15–64): **70.2%** vs. Male **78.9%** (Q2 2024).
- Employment rate for 55–64 year olds: **65.1%**, continuing a long‑term upward trend.
- Youth employment rate (15–24): **50.4%**, partly reflecting higher educational participation.

### 8.5 In‑Work Poverty
The “Survey of Income and Living Conditions (SILC) 2023” showed an **in‑work poverty rate of 5.1%** (people in employment below the poverty line), with part‑time workers and lone parents most affected.

## 9. Conclusion and Outlook

Ireland’s labour market is in a period of exceptional strength, with employment at a record high and unemployment at a near‑historic low. Flexible forms of work (remote, part‑time) have become embedded, and the self‑employed sector remains substantial. The state’s activation infrastructure reaches roughly **one‑fifth of all jobseekers**, and robust evaluation evidence shows that schemes such as JobPath, WPEP and CE improve employment prospects, particularly for those furthest from the labour market. Re‑employment happens swiftly for the majority of newly unemployed, yet challenges persist in reducing long‑term unemployment, tackling regional imbalances and ensuring that employment is of sufficient quality to lift people out of poverty. Future policy will need to focus on skills matching, in‑work progression and maintaining the high success rates of activation programmes as the labour market cycle matures.
